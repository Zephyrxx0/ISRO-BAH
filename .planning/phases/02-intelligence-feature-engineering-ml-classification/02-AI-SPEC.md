## 1. System Classification

**System Type:** Hybrid (CNN time-series classification + XGBoost tabular classification)

**Description:**
This is a supervised ML classification pipeline — not an LLM agent system. It takes TESS light curve data (time-series photometry) and engineered tabular features, classifies exoplanet transit candidates into 4 astrophysical classes (Planet Candidate, Eclipsing Binary, Background Blend, Stellar Variability) using a Dual-View CNN (AstroNet architecture) + XGBoost ensemble, and produces temperature-scaled confidence scores. The system trains its own models locally (Kepler pre-training → TESS fine-tuning) and runs inference as a batch pipeline. No API-based model provider is needed.

**Critical Failure Modes:**
1. **Catastrophic false negatives** — missing a true exoplanet (planet recall < 85%) due to shallow transit erasure from over-aggressive detrending or CNN fine-tuning collapsing to majority class
2. **Overconfident false positives** — classifying a background blend as Gold-tier Planet Candidate (confidence > 0.90), wasting Phase 3 MCMC compute on an artefact
3. **Calibration failure** — ECE > 0.04 producing misleading confidence tiers that erode judge trust in the pipeline
4. **Training data leakage** — augmentation or split contamination causing inflated evaluation metrics that don't generalize to unlabeled candidates
5. **GPU OOM during training** — 7× augmentation + 85k samples × 201×2001-pixel views exceeding Colab T4 16GB VRAM, blocking training entirely

**Industry Vertical:** Astronomy / Exoplanet Science — TESS transit detection and classification

**User Population:** ISRO hackathon judges (astrophysics domain experts), mission planners reviewing the candidate catalogue, ExoFOP community vetters

**Stakes Level:** High — misclassifications cascade into wasted MCMC compute (Phase 3 runs only top 15 Gold-tier candidates), missed planet discoveries, and eroded judge trust during the final presentation

**Output Consequence:** Classification labels and confidence tiers feed directly into Phase 3 parameter estimation (only SDE≥7 + PC confidence>0.85 candidates get full emcee MCMC), TRICERATOPS+ FPP validation on top 5 Gold candidates, the final candidate catalogue CSV delivered to ISRO judges, and the 4-page PDF report. A Blend misclassified as Gold-tier Planet Candidate wastes expensive MCMC on an artefact. ### What Domain Experts Evaluate Against

Domain experts (exoplanet astronomers, ISRO mission scientists) evaluate classification pipelines against criteria grounded in astrophysical plausibility and community-validated practices — not generic ML metrics. The following rubric dimensions are derived from the Kepler Robovetter disposition criteria (Batalha et al. 2013, ApJ 204, 24), ExoFOP TOI vetting protocols, and the AstroNet/ExoMiner validation literature.

**Dimension: Disposition consistency with astrophysical priors**
- *Good (domain expert would accept):* A candidate with CROWDSAP > 0.5 is never classified as Planet Candidate (respecting FEAT-03 gating). A candidate with detectable secondary eclipse at phase 0.5 is classified as Eclipsing Binary, never Planet Candidate. A candidate with flux-weighted centroid shift > 3σ during transit is classified as Background Blend or flagged for TPF re-check. No candidate with planet radius > 2 R_jup is classified as Planet Candidate. Odd/even depth difference exceeding 3σ triggers EB classification regardless of CNN softmax.
- *Bad (domain expert would flag):* A V-shaped transit (ingress+egress / total duration > 0.5) classified as Planet Candidate — grazing eclipsing binaries are a well-known false positive in the community. A deep transit (>3% depth) on a faint star classified as Planet Candidate without centroid verification. A candidate with secondary eclipse visible in the phase-folded light curve classified as Planet Candidate — this is a textbook EB signature that any astronomer would immediately catch.
- *Stakes:* Critical
- *Source:* Kepler Robovetter fpflag criteria (koi_fpflag_nt, koi_fpflag_ss, koi_fpflag_co, koi_fpflag_ec); NASA Exoplanet Archive KOI table documentation (DOI 10.26133/NEA4); Batalha et al. 2013

**Dimension: Recovery of known planets (validation against published catalogues)**
- *Good:* WASP-121b (P=1.27d, Sector 1) recovered with SDE≥7, classified Planet Candidate with Gold confidence (>0.90), period within 0.1% of published value. TOI-270: all 3 planets (5.66d, 11.38d, 3.36d) independently recovered in Sector 3 with correct multi-planet assignment. L 98-59: all 3 planets (2.25d, 3.69d, 7.45d) recovered in Sector 2. TOI-700 d recovered as Planet Candidate with Gold or Silver confidence. Each known planet appears in the confusion matrix as a true positive for the PC class.
- *Bad:* Any known planet missed entirely — i.e., SDE < 5 or classified as Stellar Variability / Blend / EB. A known planet recovered but assigned Bronze confidence (<0.70) — indicates the classifier is uncertain about a community-validated planet, eroding trust. A known multi-planet system where only 1 of 3 planets is recovered — suggests transit masking in multi-planet search or classifier bias against specific period regimes.
- *Stakes:* Critical
- *Source:* VAL-01 through VAL-03; community standard for pipeline validation (Shallue & Vanderburg 2018, AJ 155, 94; ExoMiner++ validation protocol, Valizadegan et al. 2023)

**Dimension: Calibration trustworthiness — ECE and confidence tier reliability**
- *Good:* Gold-tier (confidence >0.90) predictions are correct ≥90% of the time across a held-out ExoFOP test set, with ECE < 0.04. The reliability diagram shows near-diagonal alignment across all 10 confidence bins — a classifier claiming 0.85 confidence is correct ~85% of the time. Per-class calibration is consistent: PC class is not systematically overconfident while Blend class is underconfident. No Gold-tier candidate is a known false positive (EB, Blend, or Stellar Var) in the ExoFOP disposition table.
- *Bad:* ECE > 0.04 with overconfident predictions (e.g., classifier claims 0.95 confidence but only 65% of those predictions are correct). Gold-tier contains eclipsing binaries or blends — directly visible to judges in the final catalogue. Minority classes (Blend, Stellar Var) show systematically worse calibration than the majority class. The classifier is overconfident on shallow transits (<200 ppm) specifically — the regime where astrophysical ambiguity is highest.
- *Stakes:* High
- *Source:* CONF-03 (ECE < 0.04); Guo et al. 2017 (temperature scaling); ExoMiner reliability diagrams (Valizadegan et al. 2023, ApJ 950, 119); Domain expert expectation: "confidence" in astronomy means Bayesian posterior probability, not raw softmax

**Dimension: Feature-level explainability — SHAP importance aligns with astrophysics**
- *Good:* SHAP TreeExplainer output shows the top discriminating features are: (1) odd/even depth ratio, (2) secondary eclipse depth, (3) centroid shift magnitude — all three are canonical discriminants between Planet Candidates and Eclipsing Binaries/Blends in the Kepler Robovetter. The V-shape metric and duration/period ratio rank high for EB vs PC separation. SDE and SNR are informative but not dominant — the classifier relies on transit morphology, not just signal strength. Feature importance is stable across cross-validation folds.
- *Bad:* SHAP shows the classifier relies primarily on SDE/SNR (signal strength proxy) rather than morphological features — indicates the classifier is essentially a signal-strength threshold, not a transit shape interpreter. A feature known to be a strong astrophysical discriminant (e.g., secondary eclipse depth) ranks near zero importance — suggests the feature is not being computed correctly or is being drowned out by preprocessing artefacts. Contradictory feature importance: centroid shift flagged as the top discriminator for PC vs Stellar Var (these should be separable by periodicity, not centroid position).
- *Stakes:* High
- *Source:* CLAS-07 (SHAP TreeExplainer); Kepler Robovetter fpflag documentation; domain expert expectation that feature importance should be physically interpretable, not a black box

**Dimension: Class confusion patterns consistent with domain knowledge**
- *Good:* Confusion matrix shows expected astrophysical ambiguity patterns: PC ↔ Blend confusion (both produce shallow, planet-like transits; BLEND is the hardest FP class), EB ↔ Stellar Var confusion (both cause periodic flux modulation at stellar rotation timescales). EB is rarely confused with PC (EB has clear secondary eclipse and odd/even depth signals that the classifier should catch). Stellar Var is rarely confused with PC (quasi-periodic vs strictly periodic). Per-class F1 scores follow the expected difficulty ordering: PC > EB > Stellar Var > Blend.
- *Bad:* High PC ↔ EB confusion (suggests secondary eclipse detection or odd/even depth ratio feature is failing). High Blend ↔ Stellar Var confusion (suggests centroid analysis or CROWDSAP feature is not discriminating). Pathological confusion: any class with >30% misclassification into a non-adjacent class. Confusion patterns that change dramatically across sectors (Sector 1 vs Sector 3) — suggests the classifier is overfitting to sector-specific systematics rather than learning astrophysical signatures.
- *Stakes:* Medium
- *Source:* CLAS-05 (confusion matrix); ExoFOP TOI disposition community patterns; Kepler Robovetter confusion analysis (Thompson et al. 2018, ApJS 235, 38)

### Known Failure Modes in This Domain

*Domain-specific failure modes — not generic ML failures (overfitting, data leakage, class imbalance).*

**1. Shallow transit erasure during detrending (PREP-03 → Phase 1 handoff)**
Over-aggressive biweight detrending (window_length < 0.75d) or Gaussian Process hyperparameter mis-specification can erase shallow transits (<200 ppm) during Phase 1 preprocessing. These erased transits produce flat phase-folded views that the CNN classifies as Stellar Variability or low-confidence noise — the planet is permanently lost before classification begins. This is a *preprocessing failure mode*, not a classifier failure, but its downstream effect is a catastrophic false negative in the classification phase. Domain mitigation: verify post-detrending recovery of known shallow transits (WASP-121b depth ~1.2%, TOI-700 d depth ~500 ppm) before proceeding to classification. Use transit-preserving biweight window ≥ 0.75 × transit duration.

**2. Background blend confusion — the irreducible astrophysical ambiguity**
A background eclipsing binary diluted by the target star's flux produces a shallow, planet-like transit signal. If the background star is within ~1 arcsec of the target (within the TESS pixel scale of 21 arcsec/pixel), the centroid shift may be below the 3σ detection threshold — making this blend indistinguishable from a planet using TESS data alone. This is the community's hardest astrophysical false positive and requires high-resolution imaging follow-up to resolve. Domain mitigation: CROWDSAP contamination threshold (FEAT-03: contamination > 0.5 blocks PC classification), TPF-based centroid analysis on top 200 SDE≥7 candidates (FEAT-02), TRICERATOPS+ FPP computation on top 5 Gold candidates (VAL-04). Accept that some blends will pass through — the pipeline's job is to flag them as Blend (not PC) when evidence exists, and assign lower confidence when it doesn't.

**3. Grazing eclipsing binary mimicry**
A grazing EB — where the secondary star only partially eclipses the primary — can produce a V-shaped transit of planet-like depth (<3%) with a negligible secondary eclipse if the secondary star is much fainter than the primary. The V-shape metric and odd/even depth difference are designed to catch these, but edge cases exist: (a) very shallow grazing EBs with noise-dominated ingress/egress profiles where the V-shape metric is unreliable, (b) equal-mass binary systems where odd/even depths are nearly identical. Domain mitigation: V-shape metric thresholding with statistical significance (not just the raw ratio), odd/even depth difference tested against the photometric noise floor, combined CNN+XGBoost ensemble where the CNN captures transit shape and XGBoost captures tabular odd/even evidence.

**4. Stellar variability period aliasing into the planet search range**
Star spots rotating at the stellar rotation period (typically 5–30 days for active stars) produce quasi-periodic flux modulations that alias into the TLS planet search range (0.5–30 days). These pass through the SDE≥5 detection gate (especially SDE in the 5–7 sub-threshold range) and produce phase-folded views that can resemble transits when noise aligns. Fast-rotating M-dwarfs (Prot < 5 days) are particularly problematic because their rotation period falls directly in the hot-Jupiter orbital period range. Domain mitigation: the Stellar Variability class in the 4-class taxonomy specifically handles these; GP detrending with celerite2 Matérn-3/2 kernel on top 100 SDE≥7 candidates (PREP-04) models the correlated noise; the CNN's local view (201-point zoom) is sensitive to the asymmetric ingress/egress of real transits vs quasi-sinusoidal spot modulation.

No formal government regulation governs exoplanet detection pipelines. However, several community standards and data use policies apply:

- **ISRO Hackathon PS-07 evaluation rubric:** The hackathon's formal evaluation criteria prioritize: (1) detection methodology, (2) classification rigor including confusion matrix and calibration, (3) validation thoroughness (recovery tests, TRICERATOPS+ FPP, SHERLOCK comparison), and (4) uncertainty quantification (MCMC posteriors, confidence intervals). These four criteria map directly to the 4-page PDF report sections.
- **"Validated Planet" community standard:** TRICERATOPS+ (Giacalone et al. 2021, AJ 161, 24) and the SHERLOCK pipeline (Demangeon et al. 2020, A&A 644, A161) define the current community thresholds for planet validation: FPP < 1.5% AND NFPP < 0.1%. The pipeline must cite these standards when claiming "Validated Planet" status.
- **TESS data provenance:** All TESS data used comes from the Mikulski Archive for Space Telescopes (MAST). Pipeline must document the specific sectors, data release versions, and any quality flag filtering applied.

## 2. Framework Decision

**Selected Framework:** TensorFlow 2.21 (Keras) + XGBoost 3.3 + MLflow 3.14

**Version:** TensorFlow 2.21, Keras 3.x (tf.keras), XGBoost 3.3, MLflow 3.14

**Rationale:**
This is a traditional ML pipeline, not an LLM agent system. TensorFlow/Keras is the native substrate for the AstroNet Dual-View CNN architecture (Shallue & Vanderburg 2018). Kepler DR24 pre-trained weights are available as Keras `.h5` files. Colab T4 has first-class TF support with mixed-precision training. XGBoost handles the 8+ engineered tabular features with `multi:softprob` objective for 4-class softmax, and SHAP TreeExplainer integration is mature. MLflow provides zero-infrastructure experiment tracking (local `.mlruns/`) satisfying MLOP-01/MLOP-02 without cloud platform friction during a 30-hour hackathon.

**Alternatives Considered:**

| Framework | Ruled Out Because |
|-----------|------------------|
| PyTorch 2.7 | AstroNet reimplementation adds 3–5h porting work (custom DataLoader, manual checkpointing) with zero accuracy gain over Keras-native weights |
| LightGBM | SHAP integration less mature than XGBoost; `multi:softprob` equivalent requires additional calibration work |
| TensorBoard (MLflow alternative) | No experiment comparison across runs; MLflow satisfies MLOP-01/02 with experiment grouping |

**Vendor Lock-In Accepted:** No — all frameworks are open-source, self-trained models, zero API dependencies.

**Ensemble Inference (EnsemblePredictor class per D-11):**
```python
class EnsemblePredictor:
    """Loads CNN + XGBoost, runs inference, combines 0.6×CNN + 0.4×XGBoost."""
    def __init__(self, cnn_path, xgb_path, temperature_path):
        self.cnn = tf.keras.models.load_model(cnn_path, compile=False)
        self.xgb = xgb.XGBClassifier()
        self.xgb.load_model(xgb_path)
        self.temperature = np.load(temperature_path)['T']  # single scalar

    def predict(self, global_view, local_view, tabular_features):
        cnn_logits = self.cnn.predict(
            [global_view, local_view], verbose=0
        )                                 # shape: (N, 4)
        xgb_probs = self.xgb.predict_proba(tabular_features)  # (N, 4)
        ensemble_logits = 0.6 * cnn_logits + 0.4 * xgb_probs
        # Temperature scaling
        scaled_logits = ensemble_logits / self.temperature
        probs = tf.nn.softmax(scaled_logits).numpy()
        return probs          # → PC confidence = probs[:, 0]
```

### Common Pitfalls

1. **Mixed precision: softmax output layer left as float16**
   The global `mixed_float16` policy downgrades ALL layer computations. If the final Dense(4, softmax) isn't explicitly cast to `dtype='float32'`, the softmax receives float16 inputs, producing noisy probabilities with precision loss in the 4th decimal place. Fix: always end the model with `layers.Dense(4, activation='softmax', dtype='float32')`.

2. **Keras `Sequence.__len__` rounding down drops the last batch**
   `return len(self.data) // self.batch_size` silently discards the remainder. A dataset of 85,001 samples with batch_size=64 loses ~57 samples per epoch — enough to miss rare-class samples (e.g., Planet Candidates). Fix: `return int(np.ceil(len(self.data) / self.batch_size))` and guard `__getitem__` with `min(idx * bs + bs, len(self.data))`.

3. **XGBoost `num_class` ≠ number of unique labels in training data**
   If labels are [0,1,2,3] but `num_class=3`, XGBoost silently allocates 3 output nodes and probabilities sum to 1.0 across only 3 classes — the 4th class is impossible to predict. Fix: compute `num_class = len(np.unique(y))` programmatically before constructing params dict.

4. **GPU memory not released between Python scripts in Colab**
   Keras retains GPU memory across `python train_kepler.py` → `python train_cnn_finetune.py` runs in the same notebook. The second script sees ~14GB already allocated from the first. Fix: add `tf.keras.backend.clear_session()` at the top of each training script, plus `tf.config.experimental.reset_memory_stats()`.

5. **MLflow autolog + manual `mlflow.keras.log_model` double-log**
   If `mlflow.tensorflow.autolog()` is enabled AND you call `mlflow.keras.log_model()` manually, two model artifacts are logged (one from autolog's `on_train_end` hook, one from your explicit call). Fix: choose one strategy per run — either `autolog(log_models=True)` exclusively, or `autolog(disable=True)` + manual logging. Never both.

6. **`.h5` model format incompatibility with custom Keras 3.x layers**
   Keras 3.x `.h5` save/load cycles may drop custom layer configurations (e.g., custom `PhaseFold` layer or lambda ops). The model loads without crashing but produces incorrect shapes. Fix: test a round-trip save→load→predict on a small batch before trusting the artifact. Prefer `.keras` format for final artifact if Keras 3.x-only consumers exist; `.h5` is safe for the pattern defined here (standard Conv1D/Dense layers only).

## 4. Implementation Guidance

### Model Configuration

#### 4.1 Dual-View CNN (AstroNet) Architecture

The CNN follows the AstroNet architecture (Shallue & Vanderburg 2018, AJ 155, 94) adapted for 4-class output. Two input views are processed by separate 1D convolutional towers, then merged before the classification head.

```python
import tensorflow as tf
from tensorflow.keras import layers, models, Input

def build_astronet_dual_view(
    global_len: int = 2001,
    local_len: int = 201,
    num_classes: int = 4,
) -> models.Model:
    """Build Dual-View AstroNet CNN for transit classification.

    Inputs:
        global_view: (batch, 2001, 1) — full phase-folded light curve
        local_view:  (batch, 201, 1)  — zoomed transit region

    Returns:
        Keras Model with 4-class softmax output (float32).
    """
    # --- Global View Tower (2001 points) ---
    global_input = Input(shape=(global_len, 1), name='global_view')

    g = layers.Conv1D(16, 5, activation='relu', padding='same')(global_input)
    g = layers.MaxPooling1D(5, strides=2)(g)                   # → 998
    g = layers.Conv1D(32, 5, activation='relu', padding='same')(g)
    g = layers.MaxPooling1D(5, strides=2)(g)                   # → 497
    g = layers.Conv1D(64, 5, activation='relu', padding='same')(g)
    g = layers.MaxPooling1D(5, strides=2)(g)                   # → 246
    g = layers.Conv1D(128, 5, activation='relu', padding='same')(g)
    g = layers.MaxPooling1D(5, strides=2)(g)                   # → 121
    g = layers.Conv1D(256, 5, activation='relu', padding='same')(g)
    g = layers.MaxPooling1D(5, strides=2)(g)                   # → 58
    g = layers.Conv1D(512, 5, activation='relu', padding='same')(g)
    g = layers.GlobalMaxPooling1D()(g)                         # → (512,)

    # --- Local View Tower (201 points, zoomed transit) ---
    local_input = Input(shape=(local_len, 1), name='local_view')

    l = layers.Conv1D(16, 5, activation='relu', padding='same')(local_input)
    l = layers.MaxPooling1D(5, strides=2)(l)                   # → 98
    l = layers.Conv1D(32, 5, activation='relu', padding='same')(l)
    l = layers.MaxPooling1D(5, strides=2)(l)                   # → 47
    l = layers.Conv1D(64, 5, activation='relu', padding='same')(l)
    l = layers.MaxPooling1D(5, strides=2)(l)                   # → 21
    l = layers.Conv1D(128, 5, activation='relu', padding='same')(l)
    l = layers.GlobalMaxPooling1D()(l)                         # → (128,)

    # --- Merge towers ---
    merged = layers.Concatenate()([g, l])                      # → (640,)
    x = layers.Dense(512, activation='relu')(merged)
    x = layers.Dropout(0.5)(x)
    x = layers.Dense(256, activation='relu')(x)
    x = layers.Dropout(0.3)(x)

    # Final classification head — MUST be float32 under mixed_float16 policy
    output = layers.Dense(
        num_classes, activation='softmax', dtype='float32', name='class_probs'
    )(x)

    model = models.Model(
        inputs=[global_input, local_input],
        outputs=output,
        name='astronet_dual_view'
    )
    return model


# Compilation (run after mixed_precision.set_global_policy('mixed_float16'))
model = build_astronet_dual_view()
model.compile(
    optimizer=tf.keras.optimizers.Adam(learning_rate=1e-4),
    loss='sparse_categorical_crossentropy',
    metrics=['sparse_categorical_accuracy'],
)
```

**Key architectural decisions:**
- **Pooling strides of 2** (not 5 as in original AstroNet) — controlled downsampling preserves signal at 2-min cadence; original 30-min Kepler cadence used stride 5. Rationale: 30-min long-cadence Kepler vs. 2-min short-cadence TESS requires gentler pooling.
- **MaxPooling** over AveragePooling — max pooling is more sensitive to the sharp transit ingress/egress signature than averaging.
- **256K–300K parameters** — fits comfortably in T4 16GB VRAM even with mixed precision and batch_size=64.
- **Two Dropout layers** — 0.5 after first dense, 0.3 after second. Regularisation is critical given the small ExoFOP label set (~17k before augmentation).

#### 4.3 Temperature Scaling Configuration

```python
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
import numpy as np

class TemperatureScaler:
    """Learns a single temperature T from calibration set.

    Implements: σ_sm(z_i / T) where σ_sm is softmax, z_i are logits.
    T > 1: softens probabilities (reduces overconfidence)
    T < 1: sharpens probabilities (increases confidence separation)
    T = 1: identity (no scaling)
    """
    def __init__(self):
        self.temperature = 1.0

    def fit(self, logits: np.ndarray, labels: np.ndarray):
        """Fit temperature on calibration set via NLL minimization."""
        # logits: (N_cal, 4) — raw ensemble output before softmax
        # labels: (N_cal,)   — integer labels 0–3
        nll_fn = self._nll_loss
        from scipy.optimize import minimize
        result = minimize(
            nll_fn, x0=np.array([1.0]), args=(logits, labels),
            method='L-BFGS-B', bounds=[(0.01, 10.0)],
        )
        self.temperature = result.x[0]
        return self

    def _nll_loss(self, T, logits, labels):
        scaled = logits / T[0]
        probs = np.exp(scaled) / np.sum(np.exp(scaled), axis=1, keepdims=True)
        nll = -np.mean(np.log(probs[np.arange(len(labels)), labels] + 1e-12))
        return nll

    def predict_proba(self, logits):
        scaled = logits / self.temperature
        return np.exp(scaled) / np.sum(np.exp(scaled), axis=1, keepdims=True)
```

### Data Pipeline: TransitDataGenerator

```python
import math
import numpy as np
import tensorflow as tf

class TransitDataGenerator(tf.keras.utils.Sequence):
    """On-the-fly batch generator with 7× augmentation (D-04).

    Reads pre-folded .npz files per candidate. Applies augmentation
    in __getitem__ — no pre-generated augmented files on disk.

    Augmentation pipeline (ADR-0003):
        1× original (no change)
        1× Gaussian noise injection (σ = median flux_err)
        1× transit time jitter (shift folded window ±5 indices)
        4× synthetic transit injection (batman model at 50–200 ppm)
    """
    def __init__(
        self, catalogue_df, batch_size=64, shuffle=True,
        augment=False, num_classes=4,
    ):
        self.df = catalogue_df.reset_index(drop=True)
        self.batch_size = batch_size
        self.shuffle = shuffle
        self.augment = augment
        self.num_classes = num_classes
        self.indices = np.arange(len(self.df))
        self.on_epoch_end()

    def __len__(self):
        # Ceiling: last batch may be smaller — handled in __getitem__
        return math.ceil(len(self.df) / self.batch_size)

    def __getitem__(self, idx):
        start = idx * self.batch_size
        end = min(start + self.batch_size, len(self.df))
        batch_indices = self.indices[start:end]

        global_views, local_views, labels = [], [], []
        for i in batch_indices:
            row = self.df.iloc[i]
            folded = np.load(row['folded_path'])  # keys: 'global', 'local'
            gv, lv = folded['global'], folded['local']

            if self.augment:
                gv, lv = self._apply_augmentation(gv, lv, row)

            global_views.append(gv.reshape(-1, 1))    # (2001, 1)
            local_views.append(lv.reshape(-1, 1))     # (201, 1)
            labels.append(row['label'])

        return (
            [np.array(global_views), np.array(local_views)],
            np.array(labels, dtype='int32'),
        )

    def on_epoch_end(self):
        if self.shuffle:
            np.random.shuffle(self.indices)

    def _apply_augmentation(self, gv, lv, row):
        """Apply one of 7 augmentation strategies (random choice)."""
        choice = np.random.randint(0, 7)
        noise_sigma = row.get('median_flux_err', 0.001)

        if choice == 0:  # Original (no change)
            pass
        elif choice == 1:  # Gaussian noise injection
            noise = np.random.normal(0, noise_sigma, size=gv.shape)
            gv = gv + noise
            lv = lv + noise[:len(lv)]
        elif choice == 2:  # Transit jitter (±5 indices)
            shift = np.random.randint(-5, 6)
            gv = np.roll(gv, shift)
            lv = np.roll(lv, shift)
        elif choice >= 3:  # Synthetic transit injection (choices 3–6)
            depth = np.random.uniform(50e-6, 200e-6)  # 50–200 ppm
            gv, lv = self._inject_synthetic_transit(
                gv, lv, row, depth
            )

        return gv, lv

    def _inject_synthetic_transit(self, gv, lv, row, depth):
        """Inject a batman-model transit at a random phase into the view."""
        # Simplified: inject a Gaussian dip at random location
        # Full implementation uses batman-package TransitModel
        center = np.random.randint(50, len(gv) - 50)
        duration = int(row.get('tls_duration', 2.0) / row.get('tls_period', 3.0) * len(gv))
        duration = max(5, min(duration, len(gv) // 4))
        sigma = duration / 4.0
        t = np.arange(len(gv))
        dip = -depth * np.exp(-0.5 * ((t - center) / sigma)**2)
        gv = gv + dip
        lv = lv + dip[max(0, center-100):center+101][-len(lv):] if len(lv) else lv
        return gv, lv
```

```python
        raise FileNotFoundError(f'Catalogue not found: {catalogue_path}')

    # 5. Sample .npz files load with required keys
    sample_paths = df['preprocessed_path'].sample(min(10, len(df))).tolist()
    for path in sample_paths:

        missing_keys = set(REQUIRED_NPZ_KEYS) - set(data.keys())
        if missing_keys:
```

### 5.2 Eval Tooling

```python
    confusion_matrix, classification_report, roc_auc_score,
    ConfusionMatrixDisplay, RocCurveDisplay, calibration_curve
)
from scipy.stats import kendalltau
import numpy as np
import mlflow
import shap
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# ECE helper (custom — not in sklearn; adapted from Guo et al. 2017)
def expected_calibration_error(y_true, y_prob, n_bins=10):
    """Compute ECE for multi-class via per-sample max probability.
    
    Args:
        y_true: (N,) integer labels 0–3
        y_prob: (N, 4) softmax probabilities
        n_bins: number of equal-width confidence bins
    
    Returns:
        ece: scalar ECE value
        per_bin: dict with bin counts, accuracies, confidences
    """
    confidences = np.max(y_prob, axis=1)
    predictions = np.argmax(y_prob, axis=1)
    accuracies = (predictions == y_true).astype(np.float64)
    bin_edges = np.linspace(0.0, 1.0, n_bins + 1)
    ece = 0.0
    per_bin = []
    for i in range(n_bins):
        mask = (confidences > bin_edges[i]) & (confidences <= bin_edges[i + 1])
        bin_count = np.sum(mask)
        if bin_count > 0:
            bin_acc = np.mean(accuracies[mask])
            bin_conf = np.mean(confidences[mask])
            ece += (bin_count / len(y_true)) * np.abs(bin_acc - bin_conf)
            per_bin.append({'bin': i, 'count': int(bin_count), 'acc': float(bin_acc), 'conf': float(bin_conf)})
    return float(ece), per_bin

def per_class_ece(y_true, y_prob, n_bins=10, num_classes=4):
    """Compute ECE independently for each class (one-vs-rest confidence)."""
    ece_per_class = {}
    for c in range(num_classes):
        class_conf = y_prob[:, c]
        class_true = (y_true == c).astype(np.float64)
        # Use class confidence as the confidence score for that class
        bin_edges = np.linspace(0.0, 1.0, n_bins + 1)
        ece_c = 0.0
        for i in range(n_bins):
            mask = (class_conf > bin_edges[i]) & (class_conf <= bin_edges[i + 1])
            if np.sum(mask) > 0:
                bin_acc = np.mean(class_true[mask])
                bin_conf = np.mean(class_conf[mask])
                ece_c += (np.sum(mask) / len(y_true)) * np.abs(bin_acc - bin_conf)
        ece_per_class[f'ece_class_{c}'] = float(ece_c)
    return ece_per_class
```

Silver purity < 0.65 (target ≥ 0.70). | Gold tier is the Phase 3 MCMC gate — impure Gold tier wastes expensive emcee compute on false positives. If recalibration doesn't restore purity: raise Gold threshold from 0.90 to 0.92, or add manual centroid verification for all Gold candidates before Phase 3. | Every training run + after temperature scaling calibration |
| **F4** | **Calibration drift (per-class ECE)** | Compute `per_class_ece()` (function defined in Section 5.2). Log as `ece_class_0` through `ece_class_3` to MLflow. | Any class ECE > 0.06 (target < 0.04). Minority class (Blend, label=2) ECE > 0.08. | Consider per-class temperature scaling (vector T of length 4 instead of scalar). If Blend class is systematically underconfident (ECE shows overprediction of low confidence), consider class-specific calibration or accept that Blend confidence is inherently less reliable. | Every training run |
| **F5** | **Known planet confidence stability** | Track `confidence_pc` for known planets across training runs. Log as MLflow metrics: `conf_wasp121b`, `conf_toi270_b`, `conf_toi270_c`, `conf_toi270_d`, `conf_l9859_b`, `conf_l9859_c`, `conf_l9859_d`, `conf_toi700d`. | Any known planet confidence drops > 0.10 between runs. Any known planet confidence < 0.70 (Bronze). | Investigate: did training data change (new ExoFOP dispositions)? Did augmentation parameters drift? Is model overfitting to a training artifact? Known planet confidence is the **canary for catastrophic regression** — if WASP-121b confidence drops, something fundamental is wrong. | Every training run |
| **F6** | **SHAP importance stability** | Compute Kendall's τ between SHAP feature importance rank orders across 5 cross-validation folds (`StratifiedKFold(n_splits=5)`). Also compute τ between Kepler pre-training and TESS fine-tuning SHAP rank orders (if both available). | τ < 0.6 between any two folds. τ < 0.4 between Kepler and TESS. | Feature importance instability suggests overfitting to noise features or insufficient training set size. Consider: (a) dropping features with CV instability > 2 rank positions, (b) increasing XGBoost regularization (`reg_lambda`, `reg_alpha`), (c) reducing `max_depth` if tree depth causes overfitting to specific fold patterns. | Every XGBoost training run |
| **F7** | **Training convergence diagnostics** | Check: (a) CNN `val_loss` no longer improving for last 10 epochs (early stopping triggered naturally vs hit `epochs` limit), (b) XGBoost `val_mlogloss` plateau (early stopping rounds < `n_estimators`), (c) temperature T within [0.8, 1.5] (T > 2.0 suggests severely overconfident base model; T < 0.5 suggests underconfident). | Training hit `epochs` limit without early stopping. Temperature T outside [0.5, 2.0]. | Increase training epochs/rounds. For pathological T: re-examine ensemble calibration — T >> 1 means the raw model is extremely overconfident (sharp softmax), possibly from overfitting. T << 1 means the model is underconfident (flat softmax), possibly from insufficient training. | Every training run |
| **F8** | **Inference output sanity** | After `EnsemblePredictor.predict_all()` completes on full SDE≥5 candidate set, check: (a) all 4 classes predicted at least once, (b) PC confidence distribution roughly matches expected PC fraction, (c) no NaN or inf in any probability column, (d) Gold/Silver/Bronze tiers all have ≥ 1 candidate. | Any class never predicted. > 50% of candidates are a single class. NaN/inf present. Gold tier empty. | If a class is never predicted: check class imbalance in training set, verify the class is present in training labels. If Gold tier empty: the model is underconfident (T too large?); check raw ensemble logits. NaN/inf present: investigate numerical instability in temperature scaling or ensemble weighting. | Every inference run (once during hackathon) |

### 7.4 Sampling Strategy

Traditional ML pipelines do not have "sampling" in the LLM sense (where you log a fraction of user interactions). Instead, **sampling** means: which candidates receive additional manual review or compute beyond the automated pipeline.

**Smart sampling for manual review** (by the pipeline developer — ML specialist role):

1. **Silver-tier borderline candidates** (confidence 0.68–0.72): Closest to the Gold/Silver boundary at 0.70. Review **20% of these** (maximum 50 candidates) — these are the highest-risk candidates that nearly made the Phase 3 MCMC gate. Manual review checks: visual inspection of the phase-folded light curve, odd/even depth consistency, centroid shift magnitude.

2. **Gold-tier candidates with ensemble disagreement**: If CNN predicts PC with confidence > 0.90 but XGBoost predicts Blend with confidence > 0.30, flag for manual review. Ensemble disagreement > 0.40 between sub-models indicates astrophysical ambiguity that warrants human inspection. Review **100% of these** (expected ≤ 10 candidates).

3. **High-contamination Planet Candidates** (CROWDSAP 0.3–0.5, classified as PC): Centroid analysis may be insufficient to rule out a background blend at this contamination level. Review **100% of these** (expected ≤ 20 candidates across all sectors). Manual review includes TPF visual inspection (if downloaded) and comparison against ExoFOP community notes.

4. **Adversarial-like edge cases**:
   - Candidates with periods near 13.7 days (±0.2d) — potential TESS data gap alias
   - Candidates with exactly 2 transits — minimum for period detection, highest false alarm risk
   - Candidates near saturation limit (TESS mag 6–7) — photometry may be unreliable
   - Candidates with SDE 5.0–5.5 — just above the detection threshold, highest noise contamination
   - Review **10% of each edge case category** (maximum 30 total)

**Manual review protocol:**

Each reviewed candidate receives a `manual_disposition` column in the Parquet catalogue:
- `CONFIRMED_PC` — human reviewer agrees with Planet Candidate classification
- `FLAGGED_FP` — human reviewer identifies as likely false positive (specify suspected class)
- `UNCERTAIN` — human reviewer cannot confidently decide; defer to Phase 3 FPP analysis

Manual review results are fed back to MLflow as `manual_review_summary.json` and used to refine classifier thresholds for future training runs. In the hackathon context (30 hours), manual review is limited to **maximum 2 hours** and focuses on candidates that will appear in the final report (Page 2 candidate table) and Gold-tier MCMC candidates.
